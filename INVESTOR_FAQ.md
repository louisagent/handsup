# Handsup — Investor FAQ
> Prepared answers to the questions any investor or festival partner will ask.

---

## "What problem does this actually solve?"

Two people at the same show have opposite experiences: the person filming spends the whole set staring at a 6-inch screen and ends up with shaky, unusable footage nobody watches. The person next to them doesn't film and ends up with no memory at all.

Handsup solves both problems simultaneously. The person who wants to film has an audience and a purpose. Everyone else can find and download their footage after the show without having to be the one filming.

The deeper problem it solves for festivals: phone culture is actively degrading the live experience, and there's been no good answer to it until now.

---

## "Isn't this just YouTube?"

No. YouTube is a general-purpose platform where concert footage is incidental, unsearchable by event structure, constantly taken down by copyright claims, and not downloadable.

Handsup is purpose-built for this exact use case: search by artist → festival → date → download. That flow doesn't exist anywhere else.

---

## "Who uploads the videos? Classic chicken-and-egg problem."

Yes — and we have a specific plan for it. Every successful content platform solved this by seeding one specific community heavily before going broad.

Our approach:
1. **Festival partner seeding** — one partner festival gives us a shoutout to their post-event audience. One email from Laneway to 15,000 attendees = hundreds of uploads overnight.
2. **Founding Filmers program** — recruit 10-20 people per event who commit to uploading. Small community, real content.
3. **Redirect existing uploaders** — reach out to people who already uploaded concert footage to YouTube/TikTok and invite them to Handsup where it's actually discoverable.

The chicken-and-egg problem is real but solvable with one good festival partnership. That's why it's priority #1.

---

## "What stops someone from just uploading to YouTube instead?"

A few things:
- YouTube doesn't pay uploaders for concert footage (copyright strikes kill monetisation)
- On YouTube, a Tame Impala clip from Laneway 2025 is effectively unsearchable in any meaningful way
- Handsup gives uploaders a profile, stats, reputation, and eventually rewards — none of which exist on YouTube for this content type
- The specific audience (people who were at the show) is concentrated on Handsup in a way it never is on YouTube

## "What's the business model?"

**Phase 1 (now):** Free. Build the content library and user base.

**Phase 2:** Festival partnerships — $2,000–$10,000 per event for:
- Branded event page
- Analytics dashboard
- Verified crew uploads
- Post-event content report

**Phase 3:** Creator rewards programme funded by festival partners. Premium download tier. Ticketing affiliate commissions.

The B2B festival model is the most defensible revenue stream. Festivals have large marketing budgets and a genuine need for post-event content and engagement data.

## "What about copyright? Artists could shut you down."

This is the right question to ask. Our position:

1. **User-generated content from the crowd** is largely tolerated by artists and labels — they've always known fans film at shows and have generally accepted it. The legal framework is similar to YouTube's UGC policy.

2. **DMCA takedown compliance** — we'll implement standard takedown processes. Content removed promptly when rights holders request it.

3. **Artist/label partnerships** — long-term, the goal is to work *with* artists, not around them. Official footage from label-approved crew is a premium tier. Artists benefit from having their concert footage professionally curated and searchable.

4. **The Yondr angle** — phone-free shows are a growing use case where official footage is actively desired. Artists who enforce no-filming policies *want* a platform like this to exist so their fans still get the memory.

## "How big is the market?"

**Australian live music market:** ~$3.5B annually, 32M+ attendees per year.
**Global live music market:** $31B+ and growing post-COVID.

Even capturing a tiny fraction of that as a platform fee is substantial. But the more direct number: there are ~500 major festival events in Australia and internationally each year at our target scale. If each pays $5,000/year for a partnership package, that's $2.5M ARR from Australia alone.

The MVP is already built. What investment buys:
1. **Supabase + infrastructure** — real backend, video storage, CDN. ~$500/month at MVP scale.
2. **App Store accounts** — Apple ($149 AUD/year) + Google ($30 AUD one-time)
3. **Festival season activation** — travel to events, founding filmer recruitment, merch for early community.
4. **Marketing** — TikTok/Instagram content creation for launch.

Total needed to get to first 1,000 users and first paying festival partner: **~$15,000–$25,000 AUD.**

## "What's the exit?"

Platforms like this typically exit via:
- **Acquisition by a ticketing/events company** (Ticketek, Eventbrite, Live Nation) who want the content layer and community
- **Acquisition by a music streaming platform** (Spotify, Apple Music) expanding into live
- **Standalone growth** to a meaningful content platform with subscription + B2B revenue

The strategic value is the content library (exclusive, event-tagged, searchable footage) + the community of festival attendees. That's genuinely hard to replicate once built.

---

## "Why now?"

Three things converging:
1. **Phone-free concert trend** — Yondr, artist-enforced no-filming, audience frustration. The cultural moment for "put your phone down" is now.
2. **No incumbent** — the closest competitor (CrowdFlik) is dead. The market has been validated and abandoned.
3. **Post-COVID live music boom** — festival attendance is at record highs. The content creation behaviour is established; the discovery platform isn't.
